## SECTION 1 — What "job-ready" actually means (1:15 to 2:30)

Before we talk about timelines, we need to agree on what the finish line actually is. Because most people think job-ready means "I finished a course." It does not.

Job-ready means three things:

First, you can sit in an interview and answer technical questions confidently — SQL, Python, a case study, whatever they throw at you.

Second, you have 2 to 3 projects in your portfolio that show you can actually work with data end to end — not just follow a tutorial.

Third, you can explain your analysis to a non-technical person clearly. Not in code. In plain language. What is happening, why it is happening, and what to do about it.

That is the bar. Everything in this video builds toward that.

## SECTION 2 — The Skills Breakdown (2:30 to 9:00)

Now let me break down every skill you need to learn, in what order, and roughly how long each one should take. I am assuming you are putting in 2 to 3 hours of focused work a day. If you are doing more, you will move faster. If less, it will take longer. But this is a realistic baseline.

---

### 2A — Excel (Weeks 1 to 3)

I know, nobody wants to hear about Excel. But here is the reality — in most DA jobs, especially when you are starting out, you are going to use Excel more than you think.

What to learn:

Sorting, filtering, and basic formulas — SUM, AVERAGE, COUNTIF, IF statements. Then pivot tables — this is the big one. If you can build a pivot table and draw insights from it, you already know more Excel than half the applicants. Then VLOOKUP and INDEX MATCH. And finally, basic conditional formatting and simple dashboards.

You do not need to become an Excel wizard. 3 weeks of focused practice is enough. The goal is to be comfortable, not an expert.

### 2B — SQL (Weeks 3 to 8)

This is the most important skill. There is no shortcut here. Every single DA interview will test your SQL. Every single one.

**Weeks 3 to 5 — Fundamentals:**

SELECT, WHERE, GROUP BY, ORDER BY, HAVING. Basic joins — INNER JOIN, LEFT JOIN. Aggregation functions — COUNT, SUM, AVG, MIN, MAX. If you nail these, you can handle maybe 60% of what comes up in a junior interview.

**Weeks 5 to 8 — Intermediate:**

This is where you go from "I know SQL" to "I am actually good at SQL." Subqueries, CTEs (Common Table Expressions), window functions — ROW_NUMBER, RANK, LAG, LEAD. These show up in almost every mid to senior level interview question.

How to practice: do not just read about SQL. Write queries every single day. I have a set of 75 practice questions on my GitHub that cover the 10 core patterns you will see in interviews. Start there.

### 2C — Python (Weeks 6 to 11)

Let me be very clear about something. You do not need to learn Python like a software engineer. You are not building apps. You are using Python to clean data, manipulate it, and do basic analysis.

**Weeks 6 to 8 — Foundations:**

Variables, data types, loops, functions, conditionals. Setting up Jupyter Notebook. Understanding how to import libraries. This is just getting comfortable with the language.

**Weeks 8 to 11 — Pandas and data work:**

This is where it matters. Reading CSVs, filtering dataframes, groupby operations, handling missing values, merging datasets. Then basic plotting with matplotlib or seaborn — just enough to visualize what you are finding.

That is it. You do not need Flask. You do not need Django. You do not need object oriented programming. Pandas and basic data manipulation is your 80/20.

### 2D — Visualization Tool (Weeks 9 to 13)

Pick one. Tableau or Power BI. Do not learn both at the same time.

If you are targeting startups and mid-size companies, Tableau tends to be more popular. If you are targeting large enterprises and consulting firms, Power BI is often the default. Either way, the concepts transfer.

What to learn: connecting to a data source, building basic charts (bar, line, area), creating a clean dashboard with filters, and publishing your work so you can share it in interviews.

The goal is not to build the fanciest looking dashboard. The goal is to build a clear one. One that tells a story. I am making a full video on data storytelling after this one which will cover how to actually think about presenting your visuals — so make sure you are subscribed for that.

### 2E — Statistics Basics (Weeks 10 to 13)

This is the one most people either skip entirely or go way too deep on. You do not need a stats degree. But you cannot walk into an interview and not understand what a distribution is.

What you need to know: mean, median, mode — and when to use which. Standard deviation — what it tells you about your data. Correlation vs causation — this comes up in interviews all the time. Hypothesis testing basics — what a p-value is, what statistical significance means. Normal distribution — what it looks like, why it matters.

That is genuinely enough for most junior to mid level DA roles. If they ask you deeper stats questions, the role is probably more of a Data Scientist position anyway.

---

### 2F — Data Storytelling and Communication (Weeks 12 to 15)

This is the skill that nobody teaches in bootcamps but every hiring manager tests for.

Data storytelling is not about making pretty charts. It is about being able to take your analysis and explain it clearly — what is happening in the data, why it is happening, and what the business should do about it.

Communication also means: being able to write a clear summary email, being able to present findings in a meeting without reading off a script, and being able to answer follow up questions on the spot.

## SECTION 3 — Building Your Portfolio (9:00 to 10:30)

Now you have the skills. But skills without proof mean nothing. You need a portfolio.

Here is what a good portfolio looks like:

2 to 3 projects minimum. Not Kaggle Titanic. Not the same iris dataset everyone uses. Pick datasets that relate to a business problem — e-commerce, HR analytics, finance, healthcare. Something a hiring manager can relate to.

Each project should show end to end thinking. That means: you started with a business question, you cleaned the data, you explored it, you found insights, and you made a recommendation. Not just "here is a chart."

Host everything on GitHub with a clean README. Explain the problem, your approach, and your key findings. That README is often the first thing a hiring manager reads, not your code.

## SECTION 4 — Resume (10:30 to 11:15)

I made a whole video on DA resumes that has almost 20K views — I will link it above and in the description. But here is the quick version:

One page. ATS friendly format. No fancy designs or colors. Focus on impact, not just responsibilities. Instead of "used SQL to query databases" write "analyzed 500K+ transaction records using SQL to identify a 15% revenue drop in Q3, leading to a targeted marketing campaign."

Numbers are everything on a resume. If you do not have numbers from a job, use numbers from your projects.

Tailor your resume to the job description. Every single time.

## SECTION 6 — Interview Prep (11:45 to 13:00)

This is where most people underinvest. They spend months learning skills and then walk into an interview unprepared.

**SQL interview rounds:** You will get a live SQL question on a shared screen. Practice writing queries under time pressure. Not just knowing the answer, but typing it out cleanly while explaining your thought process.

**Behavioral questions:** Learn the STAR method — Situation, Task, Action, Result. Prepare 3 to 4 stories from your projects or past work that you can adapt to different questions.

**Case studies and take-home assignments:** Some companies give you a dataset and 48 hours to analyze it. This is where your portfolio practice pays off. If you have already done 2 to 3 end to end projects, this will feel familiar.

**Mock interviews:** Do them. Even if it is uncomfortable. Practice with a friend, a mentor, or someone from a community. The first time you explain your project out loud should not be in a real interview.

## SECTION 7 — The Realistic Month by Month Timeline (13:00 to 14:30)

Let me put this all together into a clear timeline. Again, this assumes 2 to 3 hours a day of focused work.

**Month 1 to 2:** Excel fundamentals, SQL basics and intermediate, start Python foundations. This is your technical base.

**Month 2 to 3:** Python Pandas and data manipulation, pick up Tableau or Power BI, start learning basic stats. You should be writing queries and cleaning data daily by now.

**Month 3 to 4:** Start your first two portfolio projects. Pick real world datasets. Practice end to end thinking — question, clean, explore, insight, recommendation. Start building your GitHub.

**Month 4 to 5:** Polish your portfolio, write clean READMEs, optimize your resume and LinkedIn. Start applying to jobs. Do not wait until you feel "100% ready" — you will never feel ready.

**Month 5 to 6:** Interview prep mode. Mock interviews, SQL under pressure, behavioral question practice. Refine your projects based on any feedback you get from applications.

Can it be faster? Yes, if you are putting in more hours or if you have some background already. Can it take longer? Also yes, and that is fine. This is not a race. The point is to be consistent and to not waste time on things that do not matter.
